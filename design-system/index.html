Foundation

Design Tokens · OKLch Color Space

Every color is an oklch() value — perceptually uniform, automatically theme-safe. The same variable names flip between dark and light via [data-theme="light"] overrides.

No hex codes in the system. No magic numbers. Every color decision is a variable. Light mode is not an afterthought — it is maintained in parallel.

Motion

Two Easing Curves · All Animations

Motion is purposeful, not decorative. --ease-out powers everything entrance-related. --ease handles transitions and state changes.

prefers-reduced-motion is respected everywhere — all scroll-triggered animations collapse to instant opacity reveals; all SVG draw animations are disabled at the stylesheet level.

Voice & Tone

Writing Principles

Direct. Declarative. Never softened. Stats stand alone — they don't need framing to feel important.

The voice is a pit board, not a press release. It knows the audience already understands racing. Context is parenthetical, not explanatory. Short sentences finish hard.

Do / Don't

✓ Do
"Eight rounds of clockwork built a 1-point lead."
Declarative. No filler. The number anchors the claim.
✗ Don't
"After an impressive performance across all eight rounds so far, the team currently holds a slim lead."
Padded, hedged, vague. "Currently" and "slim" do less than the actual number.
✓ Do
"24–29 pts every round · 203 pts · 1-pt lead"
Mono font reinforces data-first intent. The dot separator reads as a ticker, not a sentence.
✗ Don't
"They consistently scored between 24 and 29 points and now lead by one."
Loses the visual rhythm. Prose buries what data shows instantly.
✓ Do
"BZ swung higher and crashed harder."
Conversational but precise. Racing verbs. No abstract adjectives.
✗ Don't
"Team Boltus-Zerpa had some higher scoring rounds but also experienced some lower finishes."
"Some" appears twice. "Experienced" is a bureaucratic verb. Zero specificity.
✓ Do
"In multi-class, Sully and Speedysully each earn points within their own category — Gr.1 or Gr.2 — then the scores combine for the team."
Context delivered parenthetically. Em-dash rhythm. One sentence does the job.
✗ Don't
"It's important to note that this is a multi-class format, which means that the scoring works differently than what you might expect from a standard racing series."
Assumes the reader doesn't get it. Wastes 30 words on apology for complexity.

Writing Patterns

  • Stat-first opener
    Lead with the number, then explain why it matters. Never bury the lede.
    "203 pts. A 1-point lead. Two races left."
  • Em-dash context
    Use — to parenthesize technical context without breaking flow or requiring a new sentence.
    "Each driver earns points within their own category — Gr.1 or Gr.2 — then scores combine."
  • Dot separator (mono)
    In monospace contexts (moment-stat, marquee), use · to chain stats. Reads as a data stream, not prose.
    "24–29 pts every round · 203 pts · 1-pt lead"
  • Short declarative close
    End paragraphs with a short punchy sentence. Echoes the pit board aesthetic.
    "Eight rounds of clockwork built a 1-point lead."
  • No hedging
    Avoid "currently," "at this point," "potentially," "could," "might." Either it's true or say what is true.
    "Sully leads." — not "Sully is currently in the lead."
  • Section tags
    .callout-tag and .section-num use ALL CAPS + wide letter-spacing. Tags describe what follows without acting as a full sentence.
    "5/5 Rule · 2 Races Remaining (R9 & R10)"
  • Moment card titles
    Two-to-four words. Noun phrase or metaphor. Ends with a period. Never a question mark. Punchy.
    "The Clockwork Lead." · "The Swap." · "Night of the Hammer."
